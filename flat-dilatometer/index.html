Flat Dilatometer Test (DMT) in Orange, California

Knowledgeable. Thorough. Resourceful.

LEARN MORE

Downtown Orange sits on older alluvial fans with stiff clays and sands, while newer developments near the Santa Ana River encounter loose, saturated deposits. That difference in soil behavior is exactly where the Flat Dilatometer Test (DMT) provides critical data. Unlike routine boreholes, DMT measures the horizontal stress index and dilatometer modulus directly. These values tell us how the ground will respond under load. For projects in Orange, especially near the river corridor, relying only on SPT blow counts can miss important lateral stress changes. Before designing foundations or retaining walls, we pair DMT with a SPT to cross-check soil strength, or use a CPT where continuous profiles are needed.

The dilatometer measures in-situ lateral stress directly — data that SPT and lab tests cannot provide reliably in stiff clays or sands.

Process overview

I supervised a DMT campaign for a six-story residential project on Chapman Avenue. The soil profile showed interbedded silty sands and stiff clays. The dilatometer blade advanced every 20 cm, recording the lift-off pressure A and the 1.1 mm expansion pressure B. From those two readings we derived the material index, horizontal stress index, and dilatometer modulus. For clays, we estimated the undrained shear strength and overconsolidation ratio. The client needed those numbers to verify the mat foundation design.
  • Material Index (ID): identifies soil type at each depth
  • Horizontal Stress Index (KD): indicates stress history and OCR
  • Dilatometer Modulus (ED): used in settlement analysis
The whole test took one shift and produced a continuous profile down to 30 meters. No soil sampling, no transport, no lab wait.

Local context

Orange sits in a seismically active region with a 72% probability of a magnitude 6.7 or larger earthquake in the next 30 years (USGS 2023). The 1933 Long Beach earthquake caused widespread liquefaction and lateral spreading in the Santa Ana River floodplain. The DMT measures the horizontal stress index, which correlates directly with the cyclic resistance ratio for liquefaction evaluation. In older alluvial terraces near the Orange Hills, overconsolidated clays can dilate under shear, altering lateral earth pressures on retaining walls. Without the DMT, engineers rely on empirical correlations that introduce significant uncertainty in seismic design.

Q&A

How does the DMT differ from a CPT or SPT?

The DMT measures horizontal stress and modulus directly, not just tip resistance or blow count. The blade expands a steel membrane to measure the soil's reaction at two pressure thresholds. This gives you the horizontal stress index (KD), which CPT and SPT cannot measure. For settlement analysis in overconsolidated clays, DMT is more reliable.

What soil types is the DMT suitable for in Orange?

The DMT works best in clays, silts, sands, and low-plasticity gravels. It does not perform well in cemented soils, very dense gravels, or cobbles. In Orange, the alluvial deposits along the Santa Ana River and the older terrace clays near the hills are ideal candidates for DMT testing.

How much does the Flat Dilatometer Test cost in Orange?

The typical cost range for a DMT profile in Orange is between US$810 and US$900 per borehole, depending on depth, number of readings, and site access. This includes field testing, data reduction, and a summary report. Volume discounts apply for multiple test locations.

Can DMT data be used for liquefaction analysis?

Yes. The horizontal stress index (KD) from the DMT correlates with the cyclic resistance ratio (CRR) using established relationships (e.g., Marchetti 2002, Monaco 2006). In Orange, where liquefaction potential exists near the Santa Ana River, DMT provides in-situ lateral stress data that improves the reliability of liquefaction triggering evaluation compared to SPT-based methods alone.
